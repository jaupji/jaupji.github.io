Taiga

How does climate change affect taiga biomes? Climate change puts taigas in danger in different ways. Warming climate contributes to a partial thawing of the permafrost. Since this water has no place to drain, more area of the taiga is taken over by muskegs. Few trees take root. After the permanent ice caps and tundra, taiga is the terrestrial biome with the lowest annual average temperatures, with mean annual temperature generally varying from −5 to 5 °C (23 to 41 °F). Extreme winter minimums in the northern taiga are typically lower than those of the tundra. Mammals living in the taiga include foxes, lynxes, bears, minks, squirrels, while larger ones include grey wolves and their preys: caribou, reindeers and moose. In winter, wolves hunt these herbivores in packs, often dividing themselves into two groups to encircle their preys before attacking them. The plants that live in the taiga are Balsam fir, Black spruce, Conifers, Spruce, Larch, Pine, Lingonberry, Jack pine and Moss

The Taiga is known for long winters and moderate to high annual precipitation.

More than 32,000 species of insects, 130 species of fish, 85 species of mammals, and several species of birds and reptiles are found living in the taiga.
